The Unseen Thread

Three Lives, One Story

They never met. Onesimos Nesib was a freed slave who spent his life translating the Bible into Oromo. Aster Ganno was a young woman rescued from a slave ship who gave him the words he had forgotten. Gudina Tumsa was a theologian who, decades later, would carry their work into a brutal political storm.

Onesimos and Aster worked side by side at the Imkullu mission in Eritrea. Aster collected 500 traditional Oromo riddles, proverbs, and songs — preserving the soul of a people. Onesimos recognized her gift: she “possessed a real feeling for the Oromo language.” Together, they completed the first full Oromo Bible in 1899. Yet history largely forgot Aster's name.

Gudina Tumsa was born in Wallagga, the same land Onesimos and Aster had returned to in 1904. He studied theology at Luther Seminary in the United States, then became General Secretary of the Ethiopian Evangelical Church Mekane Yesus. He spoke of a “holistic theology” — faith that cares for both the soul and the suffering body.

When the Derg regime arrested him in 1979, he refused asylum in Tanzania. He quoted 2 Corinthians 5:15: “Christ died for all, that those who live should no longer live for themselves.” He was killed that night. His wife, Tsehay Tolessa, endured a decade of torture in prison.

Onesimos translated the words. Aster gave them life. Gudina died for them. This is their story — not separate, but one.

The Witnesses

Keepers of the Word

Onesimos Nesib

Translator · Missionary

c.1856 – 1931

Born Hika, meaning “translator,” he was enslaved at age four and passed through eight owners before being freed. At the Imkullu mission, he began translating the Bible into Oromo. He faced exile and persecution, but his translation — completed with Aster — became the foundation of Oromo literature.

He gave his people the Word in their own tongue.

Aster Ganno

Translator · Folklorist

c.1872 – 1964

Born free, she was enslaved and then rescued from a slave ship by the Italian navy in 1886. She was educated at Imkullu, where Onesimos recognized her gift. She compiled an Oromo dictionary, translated Bible stories, and recorded 500 traditional Oromo riddles, proverbs, and songs — preserving a culture.

Her name was nearly erased. Her work survived.

Gudina Tumsa

Theologian · Martyr

1929 – 1979

Called “the Ethiopian Bonhoeffer,” Gudina led the Mekane Yesus Church through revolution. He developed a holistic theology that refused to separate spiritual faith from social justice. Arrested three times by the Derg, he refused to flee. He was killed on 28 July 1979.

A responsible Christian does not court martyrdom — but does not flee from it.
